PROBLEM
Data visualizations can often times be too noisy and congested to expose meaningful results. Our goal was to improve Watson's interactive data visualization tool. As this is quite an undertaking, we decided our deliverable would be a referential design guide, where we break down and analyze each element of the ubiquitous hairball data visualization in hopes to find a few major insights to move forward and rethink what a data visualization is. This was just the first step for improving Watson's data visualization tool.

INITIAL USER FLOW
We created an initial user flow based off the intention that the user starts their search from a narrow, focused selection of nodes, and then broadens their exploration from there.




USER TESTING
Our front end dev coded a prototype, and with the leadership of our main design researcher Jo-Yee, we tested it out on 50+ researchers/analysts from legal, finance, food, and energy industries. One insightful research asset that came out of this user testing was a user journey map shown below, where we mapped out the peaks and lows of the user's journey and compared our expectations to what we perceived from our user. In this exercise we found that users were having a particularly hard time expanding links in between nodes and with the zoom and multiselect feature.

SOLUTION
Unlike a typical hairball data visualization where you start with a lot and slowly narrow your search, the goal of the data visualization in Watson Discovery Advisor was to "find a needle in a haystack". With this concept, we designed around the notion that your search would bring you to one or a few crucial nodes, where you could expand and explore from there. Our final deliverables consisted of a design guide that included responsive layout instructions, UI scheme, canvas rules, accessibility options, and more. In the end, we presented and handed off our deliverables to the Watson design language team to continue where we left off.
